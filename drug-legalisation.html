Drug Legalisation

Legalisation of drugs for consenting adults, combined with full personal responsibility, tort liability, and market-driven regulation.

Drug Legalisation Policy

Core Principle

Adults own their own bodies. The state has no legitimate authority to criminalise the private, consensual ingestion of substances by competent adults. Prohibition has failed both practically and morally — it creates black markets, enriches criminals, corrupts law enforcement, and diverts resources from real crime.

Legalisation Framework

All drugs are legal for adults aged 18 and over, subject to:

  • Strict age restrictions (sale to minors remains a serious criminal offence)
  • Full tort and civil liability — producers, sellers, and users are responsible for harm caused to others
  • No state licensing or monopoly — private markets with insurance and certification
  • Public consumption regulated by property owners and local nuisance laws
Dovetailing with Criminal Justice

This policy works directly with our criminal justice reforms:

  • Police resources (via FLIP) are freed up to focus on actual victims and initiation of force rather than victimless crimes.
  • Harm to others (driving under the influence, violence, supply to minors) remains heavily punished under Common Law and criminal statutes.
  • Insurance markets and tort law handle most day-to-day risk and accountability more efficiently than prohibition ever could.
Regulation Without Prohibition

Quality, purity, and safety are enforced through:

  • Private certification and reputation markets
  • Strict product liability under tort law
  • Insurance pricing that reflects real risk
  • Full transparency and consumer information
The Goal

To treat adults as responsible agents, dismantle the destructive prohibition regime, reduce crime and corruption, and refocus the justice system on protecting people from real harm rather than policing personal choices.
