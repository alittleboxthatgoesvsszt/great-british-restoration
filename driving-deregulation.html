Driving & Vehicle Deregulation

Replacing state licensing, MOTs, and detailed regulation with personal responsibility, tort law, insurance markets, and Common Law.

Driving and Vehicle Regulation Reform

Core Principle

The state should not micromanage personal travel. Adults are responsible for their own safety and the safety of others. Most current driving regulations are prior restraint rather than remedies for actual harm and should be repealed.

Key Reforms
  • Abolish compulsory state licensing and testing for private vehicles.
  • Replace government MOTs with voluntary or insurance-driven safety standards.
  • Liability governed by Common Law and tort — drivers and owners are fully responsible for harm caused.
  • Insurance markets set practical standards (most insurers will require proof of competence and vehicle roadworthiness).
  • Non-strategic roads are privatised or locally managed (see Roads Policy and PRUNE).
Enforcement

Unsafe or reckless behaviour is handled through:

  • Criminal law for dangerous driving causing harm
  • Civil tort claims for damages
  • Insurance companies refusing coverage or charging much higher premiums for high-risk drivers
Advantages
  • Reduces bureaucratic burden and costs
  • Aligns incentives through personal liability and insurance pricing
  • Allows innovation in vehicle design and mobility solutions
  • Focuses enforcement on actual harm rather than paperwork
The Goal

A freer, more efficient transport system where individuals bear responsibility for their actions, insurance markets set sensible standards, and the state intervenes only when there is clear initiation of force or serious negligence.
