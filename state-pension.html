Great British National Restoration Framework

State Pension Reform

The orderly, humane wind-down of the unsustainable state pension system and transition to genuine personal and family responsibility.

Ending the State Pension

Core Principle

The state pension, as currently structured, is actuarially unsustainable and represents an enormous unfunded liability passed on to future generations. In a minarchist framework, the state should not be in the business of forcing people to save through it or promising benefits it cannot reliably deliver.

Transition Plan

The state pension will be phased out as part of the WEAN programme and STEP transition:

  • Current pensioners (already receiving payments) will continue to receive their full entitlement for life, protected by law.
  • Those aged 45 and over at the start of the transition will receive a tapered guarantee.
  • Those under 45 will have their future state pension rights progressively reduced and eventually eliminated.
  • All National Insurance contributions related to the state pension will be abolished and replaced by private saving and general PETS system.
Replacement Model

People will be expected to provide for their own retirement through:

  • Private pensions and savings
  • Family support structures (strengthened by private marriage contracts and STORK)
  • Continued work where desired (no mandatory retirement age)
  • Charitable and community provision for those who genuinely cannot provide for themselves
Why This Is Necessary

The current pay-as-you-go system is a demographic Ponzi scheme. With falling birthrates and rising life expectancy, it places an ever-growing burden on younger workers. Continuing this model is both immoral to future generations and fiscally unsustainable. An orderly wind-down protects current pensioners while ending the transfer of massive liabilities onto the young.

The Goal

To end the state’s role as a pension provider, restore personal and family responsibility for retirement, and eliminate one of the largest long-term fiscal threats to Britain’s solvency — all while honouring commitments already made to current pensioners.
